Nepal's Melting Giants:
A Himalayan Crisis

← Back to Home

Key Facts: Nepal's glaciers are retreating at an alarming rate of 10 to 60 meters per year, while the country contributes only 0.027% of global greenhouse gas emissions.

In the heart of the Himalayas, Nepal's majestic glaciers are disappearing at an unprecedented rate, despite the country's minimal contribution to global carbon emissions. This mountainous nation, home to eight of the world's fourteen highest peaks, is witnessing the devastating effects of climate change firsthand.

The Scale of Loss

Recent studies have revealed alarming statistics about Nepal's glacial retreat:

  • Over 30% of Nepal's glacial ice has vanished in just three decades
  • The average temperature in the Himalayas is rising by 0.6°C per decade, far exceeding global averages
  • Nepal has lost 24% of its glacier area between 1977 and 2010
  • The number of glacial lakes has increased by 11% since 1977 due to rapid ice melt

Impact on Water Security

Critical Impact: Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs) have increased in frequency, threatening downstream communities and infrastructure. Nepal now has over 2,000 glacial lakes, with 21 identified as potentially dangerous.

The Human Cost

While Nepal's carbon footprint remains minimal (0.027% of global emissions), its people are bearing the brunt of climate change impacts:

  • Increased risk of natural disasters affecting mountain communities
  • Threats to food security due to changing precipitation patterns
  • Loss of traditional livelihoods dependent on predictable water supplies
  • Growing concerns about long-term water availability for agriculture and drinking

Nepal's Response

Despite limited resources, Nepal is taking steps to address these challenges:

  • Implementation of the National Adaptation Plan of Action
  • Development of early warning systems for GLOFs
  • Community-based adaptation programs in vulnerable regions
  • International advocacy for climate justice and support

Call to Action: Nepal's story exemplifies the global climate crisis's unfair impact on developing nations. While contributing minimally to global emissions, countries like Nepal face disproportionate consequences, highlighting the urgent need for global climate justice and action.
